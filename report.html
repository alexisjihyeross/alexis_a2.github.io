Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Question 1: What types of properties are being sold in Boston, specifically in the luxury units? (ie are they 1-bedroom units or family units?) I am wondering because the Boston Globe article mentioned that only 16% of One Dalton’s units are owners’ primary homes, and so I wonder if the units are small units meant for individuals or for families.
  2. Question 2: How have the uses of properties and identities of property buyers changed over time? For instance, are properties being increasingly bought by corporate buyers as long-term investments? As short term flips? I am curious because the Boston Globe article talked about the phenomenon of large corporations buying housing stock and selling it for profit, particularly recently, and I am curious to see if this data would reflect that trend. After reading the ProPublica article for A1 discussing that many buildings in the US are owned by a small number of large private equity firms, I wonder if we would observe the same thing for luxury buildings in Boston—ie that a few companies/buyers are buying a large share of the properties.
  3. Question 3: Where are luxury buildings being bought? I am interested in this question because the Boston Globe article talked about how the affordable housing build by One Dalton's developers was 1.5 miles away from One Dalton; such distance suggests that we would see property inequities geographically distributed, with certain areas of Boston having higher concentrations of luxury buildings than others.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In this exploratory data analysis, we explore 1) the kinds of properties that are being sold in Boston, with a focus on luxury units, 2) how the types of property purchases have changed over time, and 3) where luxury buildings are being bought. For each of these questions, we first check the data quality and overall distribution; these checks allow us to identify and correct data entry errors (e.g. incorrect zip codes) and to filter out data not relevant to our questions (e.g. non-residential properties).

We find that luxury units are primarily 2-3 bedroom units, and that units with more bedrooms have higher average flip horizons. We also find that the number of luxury residential units sold has steadily increased over time, and that the proportion of units bought by an LLC has increased over time. We find that both LLCs and non-LLCs are making positive average price differences from flipping luxury residential units, and that LLCs are making more money from flipping luxury residential units than non-LLCs. We also find that the average number of properties owned by buyers has increased over time, and that LLCs have owned more properties than non-LLCs since 2018. Lastly, we find that the most expensive residential units are being sold in the downtown area, while the least expensive residential units are being sold in the outskirts of Boston.

Together, these findings suggest that the luxury residential market is growing, and that it is increasingly being dominated by companies rather than individuals. These findings align with the Boston Globe article on Towers of Wealth, which talked about the increasing number of luxury residential units being built in Boston, amidst a decreasing number of non-luxury residential units available to the population.
